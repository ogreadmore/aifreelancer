⚡ Core Tier — AI‑Powered Team Training

Minimal integrations, fast setup, small footprint. Launch a private training assistant, keep comms simple, and measure the basics.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We turn your current SOPs and how‑tos into a private Q&A assistant. We start with the most used docs, wire quick alerts for unanswered questions, and share a simple monthly snapshot so everyone can see improvements.

  • Capture & upsert: Bring SOPs from Notion/Docs; ingest and index for fast answers.
  • Quality & guardrails: Short, on‑brand answers; clear hand‑offs if unsure.
  • Comms & alerts: Speed‑to‑answer alerts; quiet hours; owners assigned by topic.
  • Report: Self‑service rate, common gaps, ramp trend.
Newbie guide: How this feels end‑to‑end

Story mode: Imagine you’re the team lead welcoming a new hire. Your goal is simple: when they ask “How do I do X?”, they get a short, correct answer with a link to the official SOP, and if the bot isn’t sure, it politely asks for help and tells you. You’ll start small, fix rough edges quickly, and then make it available to everyone.

What you’ll actually do: you’ll pick the 10–30 most used SOPs, drop them into the assistant, run through a list of common questions, and patch any weak spots by improving the SOPs or adding clarifying notes. Then you’ll invite a tiny pilot group, fix issues, and roll out to the full team with simple metrics everyone understands.

  1. List your top questions (onboarding, passwords, refunds, tool access). Ask managers for their top 5 each.
  2. Collect the latest SOP links. If multiple versions exist, pick the newest and archive the rest.
  3. Load the SOPs into the assistant and ask those top questions. Note any fuzzy or missing answers.
  4. Fix gaps: add a missing step or link right into the SOP. Keep answers short and on‑brand.
  5. Turn alerts on during work hours. If the bot can’t answer, it notifies the assigned owner.
  6. Invite 5–10 people for a 1‑week pilot. Watch which topics fail and patch them daily.
  7. Roll out to the whole team. Share a one‑page monthly snapshot: self‑service %, top gaps, next 3 fixes.

Acceptance checklist: (a) answers include a source link, (b) unknowns route to an owner, (c) logs show every question, (d) self‑service ≥ 60% after the first month.

Common pitfalls: old SOPs, ambiguous steps without screenshots, and alerts firing at night. Fix by archiving drafts, adding a single screenshot or example, and setting quiet hours.

💬 Communication & Reporting

Weekly status email; one‑page plan after discovery; end‑of‑month KPI snapshot. Plain‑English and visual.

  • Cadence: Weekly updates (Done, Next, Risks/Needs). Slack/Email agreed.
  • Visibility: Lightweight Kanban board (To‑Do/Doing/Done). Client access.
  • Reporting: Monthly: self‑service, ramp proxy, unanswered topics, actions.
  • Decision log: Tiny record of key choices with dates.
Newbie guide: What do we report?

Story mode: Keep reports boringly simple so everyone reads them. One page only: a tiny chart for self‑service rate, a list of the top unanswered topics, and the three changes we’ll make next month (with owners and dates).

  1. Export the self‑service % and speed‑to‑answer from the dashboard.
  2. Open the log, sort by unanswered or escalated, and group by topic (e.g., “benefits”, “refunds”).
  3. Pick the top 3 topics to fix. Write exactly what we’ll change (add a step, add a link, adjust routing).
  4. Assign an owner and a due date for each change. Put them on the Kanban board.

Tip: include one real example (question → answer link) so stakeholders see the quality.

Step 1 — Kickoff & Goals

Newbie guide: What happens in kickoff?

Story mode: In 45 minutes, you’ll agree on goals, KPIs, and guardrails. Start by writing the primary goal in one sentence (“new hires can answer common questions in under 30 seconds”). Then pick 2–3 KPIs and assign owners.

  1. Goal: faster ramp and fewer interruptions. Confirm what “done” means after 60 days.
  2. KPIs: choose self‑service %, speed‑to‑answer, and logging coverage.
  3. Guardrails: topics to avoid (legal advice, personal HR issues), brand voice, and escalation paths.
  4. Quiet hours: set the local time window when alerts must not fire.
  5. Inputs: list the top 10–30 SOPs and who owns each of them.

Step 2 — Content Intake (Capture → Upsert)

Newbie guide: Importing and cleaning

Story mode: Work from a simple spreadsheet: columns for URL, owner, topic, and status. Paste in the latest SOP links, strike through drafts, and tag each one by topic so people can find things later.

  1. Open each SOP; confirm it’s the latest. If not, link to the final version and archive the old one.
  2. De‑dupe: treat same URL with small edits as one record; keep only the newest.
  3. Tag: Onboarding, Access, Sales, Support, Finance, etc. Add team/intent if useful.
  4. Normalize contacts: if you collect phone numbers, convert to E.164; for emails, lowercase and trim.
  5. Upsert: load or update the SOP into the assistant so it’s searchable and cited.

Done when: your sheet has no drafts, every SOP has an owner, and search finds the right doc by topic keywords.

Step 3 — Assistant Setup

Newbie guide: Configure behavior

Story mode: Set the tone first: “friendly, concise, cite source”. Add a rule: when not sure, say “I’m not certain” and route with the topic owner tag. Turn on alerts 9–5 local time, weekdays only.

  1. Answer style: 2–4 sentences + a link to the SOP; no long essays.
  2. Fallbacks: apologize briefly, ask a clarifying question, or route to owner.
  3. Routing map: topic → owner email or channel. Test one unknown and confirm alert delivery.
  4. Logging: confirm each answer and each route is written to the log with a timestamp.

Step 4 — Email Auth & Notifications

Newbie guide: Email setup and privacy

Story mode: You’ll send your IT admin the DNS records to add (SPF, DKIM, DMARC). Once they’re live, send a test notification to yourself and check the headers say “authenticated”. Finally, make sure logs do not store sensitive info.

  1. Copy DNS records from your email provider and open a ticket with IT.
  2. Wait for propagation (often minutes, sometimes hours). Verify with an email tester.
  3. Turn on quiet hours and verify alerts stop at night and resume in the morning.
  4. Open the log viewer and confirm emails/phone numbers are masked unless strictly required.

Step 5 — Pilot & QA

Newbie guide: Pilot run

Story mode: Choose a small, friendly group. Ask them to use the assistant daily for 5–10 tasks. Meet mid‑week for 15 minutes to review what failed and fix it the same day.

  1. Prep: share a list of sample tasks (“reset Okta”, “refund flow”, “request laptop”).
  2. Capture: star any answer that felt slow or unclear, and paste the question into the backlog.
  3. Patch: update the SOP or add a clarifying bullet, then re‑test immediately.
  4. Sign‑off: when 90% of pilot tasks work first‑try, move to launch.

Step 6 — Launch & Measure

Newbie guide: After launch

Story mode: For the first week, check the dashboard daily. Anything that fails twice gets a fix within 48 hours. At the end of the month, publish a one‑page snapshot and refresh the backlog.

  1. Daily: scan unanswered topics; assign and fix the top two.
  2. Weekly: confirm self‑service % is trending up; spot‑check 5 logs for tone and citations.
  3. Monthly: share the snapshot, close finished items, and pick the next 3 changes.

🚀 Enhanced Tier — AI‑Powered Team Training

Add guided workflows, routing and SLA timers, enrichment, and dashboards. Connect Slack/Teams and LMS; enable bi‑directional sync.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We introduce standard workflows with routing and SLAs, enrich entries with context (time‑boxed), and draft training content with AI for human approval. We sync with LMS/CRM where needed and add a dashboard by topic/intent.

  • Workflows & SLAs: Route by team with timers and escalations.
  • Enrichment & scoring: Add context (role, team) and flag fast‑lane thresholds.
  • AI‑drafted copy: Human approvals for updates and sequences.
  • Sync: Bi‑directional with LMS/CRM; idempotency + retries/backoff.
Newbie guide: How Enhanced differs from Core

Story mode: Think of Enhanced as the “guided tour” version. You’ll add buttons for common tasks (request access, refund steps, close a ticket), make sure each path has the right questions, and connect Slack/Teams so actions reach the right people fast. You’ll also set timers (SLAs) so nothing sits idle.

  1. Build mini‑menus for top intents (Onboarding, Access, Policies, Sales FAQs).
  2. For each path: ask only what’s needed (name, email, team) and validate inputs.
  3. Wire routes: People Ops → people‑ops channel; IT → helpdesk; Sales → sales‑ops.
  4. Set timers: e.g., Access requests in 4 business hours → escalate to manager.
  5. Let AI draft content changes, but require a human approver to publish.

Outcome: users click less, wait less, and your team gets clean, validated requests.

💬 Communication & Reporting

Weekly updates; integration change log; monthly deck with self‑service by topic and SLA compliance.

  • Dashboard link; conversion to self‑service by intent/segment.
  • UAT plan and sign‑off before launch.
  • Retries/backoff and idempotency documented for integrations.
Newbie guide: What’s in the monthly deck?

Story mode: Your deck has five tiny parts: (1) wins, (2) misses, (3) SLA compliance, (4) self‑service by topic, (5) next month’s 3 actions with owners.

  1. Wins: “Onboarding self‑service up 12% after adding screenshots.”
  2. Misses: “SLA missed for Access after 5pm — we’ll add an after‑hours rotation.”
  3. SLA chart: by intent and by team; highlight any repeats.
  4. Self‑service by topic: top 5 categories, trend vs last month.
  5. Actions: who will do what by when; keep it to three.

Step 1 — Discovery & Routing

Newbie guide: Routing and SLAs

Story mode: Draw a simple map: intent → destination → timer. Then test it like a user.

  1. List intents: Access, Payroll, Benefits, Sales Requests, Product FAQ.
  2. Destination: pick the channel or system for each (Slack channel, ticket queue, inbox).
  3. Timer: set the max wait (e.g., 4h for Access, 24h for Policies) and who to escalate to.
  4. Test: submit a fake request for each intent. Confirm the message, assignee, and timer.

Step 2 — Enrichment & Scoring

Newbie guide: Enrichment basics

Story mode: Enrichment should be tiny and time‑boxed. Pull just enough data (team, location, manager) to make routing smarter — nothing more.

  1. Decide the two fields that help routing most (often team and location).
  2. Configure a quick lookup (e.g., HRIS) with a 1–2s timeout and a safe fallback.
  3. Apply fast‑lane: urgent intents or VIP teams jump the queue within SLA rules.
  4. Mask PII in logs; store only what you absolutely need.

Step 3 — Integrations

Newbie guide: Safe integrations

Story mode: Use the “belt and suspenders” approach: unique IDs + retries + timeouts + dead‑letter logging.

  1. Create an idempotency key per request (UUID). Store it for 24–48 hours.
  2. On 429/5xx, backoff 1s → 2s → 4s, stop after 3 tries, and log the failure.
  3. Test in a sandbox and verify no duplicate records are created.
  4. Capture request/response samples for the UAT pack.

Step 4 — UAT & Workflows

Newbie guide: What is UAT?

Story mode: UAT is a dress rehearsal. You’ll walk through 30–50 cases, including bad ones, and tick them off in a spreadsheet.

  1. Good paths: successful requests per intent.
  2. Bad paths: 429/5xx/timeouts and wrong inputs (bad email, empty fields).
  3. Review: if anything fails, fix it and rerun that case before launch.
  4. Sign‑off: all must be green; attach logs and screenshots to the UAT sheet.

🏢 Enterprise Tier — AI‑Powered Team Training

Harden for scale: SSO/RBAC + SCIM, maker‑checker approvals, audit logs, feature flags with canary rollout, warehouse sync and DLP, observability and SLOs, incident runbooks, and monthly evaluation packs.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We integrate identity and provisioning, add strong approvals, track every change, and control rollouts with feature flags. We sync structured events to your warehouse with lineage and DLP, monitor costs and errors, and run to SLOs with incident playbooks.

  • Identity: SSO/RBAC + SCIM user and group provisioning.
  • Controls: Maker‑checker approvals; audit logs; change calendar.
  • Rollouts: Feature flags with canary (5% → 25% → 100%) and rehearsed rollback.
  • Data: Warehouse sync; lineage; DLP and retention windows.
  • Ops: Observability (logs/traces/cost), SLOs/error budgets, incident runbooks.
Newbie guide: Enterprise in plain English

Story mode: Picture the assistant as a regular app on your corporate network. People log in with SSO, their team controls what they can see, and any sensitive change requires one person to build and another to approve. New features roll out to 5% first, then more, while we watch metrics. If anything goes wrong, we flip a switch to roll back.

  1. Connect to Okta/Azure AD. Confirm groups map to access (e.g., HR → HR paths only).
  2. Turn on SCIM so accounts auto‑provision and de‑provision. Test offboarding.
  3. Enable audit logs and store them in your SIEM for safekeeping.
  4. Create a feature flag for any big change. Roll out 5% → 25% → 100% with guardrails.
  5. Send events to the warehouse with lineage. Apply DLP and retention rules.

Result: controlled access, traceable changes, safe rollouts, and clear visibility for leaders.

💬 Communication & Reporting

Weekly project sync; monthly executive evaluation pack; change advisory board (as needed).

  • Evaluation pack: SLOs, costs, adoption, incidents, risks, roadmap.
  • Audit log exports and evidence archive.
Newbie guide: What leaders see

Story mode: Executives want signal, not noise. Your packet answers: Are we on‑track for SLOs? What did we ship? What broke? What’s next?

  1. SLOs: availability, latency (p95), error rate vs budget.
  2. Adoption: active users and self‑service trends by org.
  3. Incidents: count, time‑to‑resolve, top root causes, and fixes.
  4. Costs: usage and unit costs; any big drivers called out.
  5. Roadmap: the next 3 changes and their risks/mitigations.

Step 1 — Identity & Access

Newbie guide: Single sign‑on

Story mode: Start with SSO and SCIM. Your identity team gives you a client ID/secret or a SAML config. You map groups to roles, and SCIM keeps accounts in sync.

  1. Configure SSO in a test environment; add a test user to an access group.
  2. Map roles: Viewer, Editor, Approver; tie them to your IdP groups.
  3. Enable SCIM and create a test account via provisioning; then de‑provision it.
  4. Confirm audit logs record login, role changes, and de‑provision events.

Step 2 — Approvals & Change

Newbie guide: Safe changes

Story mode: Treat changes like air traffic control. One person builds, another approves, a flag controls exposure, and you can roll back in one click.

  1. Create a change ticket with the why, the flag name, and rollback steps.
  2. Run a canary: 5% for 2h → 25% for 6h → 100% if error rate and latency are green.
  3. Document outcomes in a change log; link to dashboards and logs.

Step 3 — Data & Warehouse

Newbie guide: Data and privacy

Story mode: Build a thin, reliable pipe of events to your warehouse, with clear names and privacy rules.

  1. Define an event schema (who, what, when, where); version it.
  2. Add lineage: store the source system and processing steps for each event.
  3. Apply DLP: mask PII; drop anything not required; set retention windows by policy.
  4. Test queries: ensure analysts can answer common questions with the data set.

Step 4 — Observability & Incidents

Newbie guide: What we watch

Story mode: Think three dashboards: reliability, performance, and cost. Tie alerts to SLOs and keep runbooks short.

  1. Reliability: error rate, failed integrations, alert fatigue.
  2. Performance: p95 latency, time‑to‑first‑answer, queue depth.
  3. Cost: usage per team, cost per question, outliers.
  4. Runbooks: 10 steps or less; include links to graphs and rollback flag.
