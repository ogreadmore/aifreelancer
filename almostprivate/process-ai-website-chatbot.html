Part 1: What & Why (Client & Manager View)

Quick Story — What will happen

Overview: We will build a smart assistant for your website. It will give visitors instant answers to their questions, 24/7. First, we'll teach the AI using your website's content. Then, we'll design the conversation to be helpful and on-brand. We'll test it carefully to make sure it's safe and accurate before putting it on your site. Finally, we'll launch it on one page, see how it does, and then roll it out everywhere.

What each step means (plain):

  • Gather knowledge: We'll collect information from your key website pages, like FAQs, services, and pricing. This becomes the chatbot's "brain."
  • Design the conversation: We'll decide what the chatbot says to greet people, how it answers questions, and when it should offer to connect them with a human.
  • Build the bot: We'll feed the knowledge into the AI and set up the rules for the conversation.
  • Test drive: We'll ask the chatbot lots of questions internally to make sure it's helpful, accurate, and doesn't say anything strange.
  • Launch slowly: We'll turn the chatbot on for just one page first, watch how visitors use it, and make sure everything is working perfectly before we add it to other pages.

For you — the person doing the work: Think of this as a clear checklist. Do these simple actions in order:

  • Define the goal with the client (is it for getting more leads or for customer support?).
  • Scrape the website content and use it to create a knowledge base (this is called a vector database).
  • Write a "system prompt" that tells the AI how to behave (e.g., "You are a friendly assistant for Brand X...").
  • Run an evaluation script with 20-30 common questions to test the chatbot's accuracy.
  • Embed the chatbot widget on a staging site to test the user experience.
  • Go live on one page, monitor the analytics for 48 hours, then roll out to the rest of the site.

For the client — what it should feel like:

  • Easy: They just need to point you to the right pages on their website and approve the chatbot's personality.
  • Instant: Their website visitors get immediate answers instead of having to search or wait for an email reply.
  • Safe: The chatbot is carefully tested to be on-brand and avoid giving incorrect or harmful information.
  • Insightful: The client gets reports on what their customers are asking, providing valuable insights.

Step 1 — Kickoff & Goals

Newbie guide: Why we define goals first

Before we build anything, we need to know what we're aiming for. This first step is about deciding the chatbot's main job.

Analogy: Think of it like hiring a new employee. You need to write a job description first. Are they a salesperson or a receptionist?

  • Lead Generation: Is the chatbot's main job to identify potential customers and ask for their contact information?
  • Customer Support: Or is its main job to answer common questions and help existing customers, so your human team has more time?

Defining this helps us make all the other decisions. A "sales" bot will be more proactive about asking questions, while a "support" bot will be more focused on providing accurate answers from the knowledge base.

Step 2 — Knowledge Base

Newbie guide: What is a Knowledge Base?

What this is: The Knowledge Base is the chatbot's "brain." It's the collection of all the information we want the chatbot to know. We don't just copy-paste the whole website; we have to prepare the information so the AI can understand it.

  • Chunking: We break down long pages of text into small, focused paragraphs or "chunks." Think of it like creating individual flashcards for the AI to study, instead of giving it a whole textbook at once.
  • Vectorizing: This is a fancy word for turning the text chunks into numbers (vectors). This process allows the AI to understand the *meaning* and *context* of the words, not just the words themselves. It's how the AI can find the right answer even if the user's question uses slightly different wording.

Why it matters: A well-built knowledge base is the secret to an accurate and helpful chatbot. If the information is clean and well-organized, the chatbot will give great answers. If it's messy, the chatbot will be confused.

Step 3 — Design & Prompting

Newbie guide: What is a System Prompt?

What this is: The System Prompt is a set of instructions we give the AI that it reads before every single conversation. It's like a director giving an actor their motivation before a scene. It defines the chatbot's personality, its rules, and its goals.

An example System Prompt might start like this:

"You are a friendly and helpful assistant for 'Awesome Inc.' Your goal is to answer questions using ONLY the information provided. Be concise. If you don't know the answer, say 'I'm not sure about that, but I can connect you with our team.' Never make up answers."

Why it matters: This is one of the most powerful tools we have to control the chatbot's behavior. A good system prompt ensures the chatbot stays on-brand, follows the rules, and is genuinely helpful to users, rather than just being a generic AI.

Step 5 — Testing (Evaluation)

Newbie guide: How we test the chatbot

What this is: Before the chatbot talks to real customers, we put it through a tough testing process. This is like a final exam for the AI.

  • Evaluation Set (The Written Test): We create a list of the most important questions a customer might ask (e.g., "What are your prices?", "What are your hours?"). We then ask the chatbot these questions and grade its answers.
  • Red Teaming (The Trick Questions): This is where we try to trick the chatbot on purpose. We might ask it for a discount it's not allowed to give, or ask it something about a competitor. This helps us make sure its safety rules are working.

Why it matters: This is the most important step for building trust. Thorough testing ensures the chatbot is reliable, safe, and provides real value. It prevents the chatbot from giving wrong answers ("hallucinating") or going off-brand, which could damage the client's reputation.

Glossary (Newbie‑friendly)

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): The technique of finding relevant information first (retrieval) and then using it to generate a better answer.

Knowledge Base: The collection of information (from your website, PDFs, etc.) that the chatbot uses to answer questions.

Vector Database: A special type of database that stores information based on its meaning, allowing the AI to find relevant content even if the words don't match exactly.

Chunking: The process of breaking down large documents into smaller, more manageable pieces for the AI.

System Prompt: The master set of instructions that tells the chatbot its personality, rules, and goals.

Containment Rate: The percentage of conversations the chatbot handles successfully without needing to escalate to a human.

Hallucination: When an AI model makes up facts or information that is not based on its knowledge source. Our goal is to minimize this.

2) Knowledge Base Pipeline

Indexing Pseudocode

pages = scrape_sitemap('https://client.com/sitemap.xml')
for url in pages:
  content = extract_text(url)
  chunks = chunk_text(content, size=512, overlap=50)
  for chunk in chunks:
    embedding = generate_embedding(chunk)
    vector_db.upsert(
      id=hash(url + chunk),
      vector=embedding,
      metadata={'url': url, 'text': chunk}
    )

3) RAG & Prompting

Retrieval Flow

  1. User sends query.
  2. Query is converted to an embedding.
  3. Vector DB is searched for top 3-5 most similar chunks (the "context").
  4. System prompt, context, and query are sent to the LLM.
  5. LLM generates an answer based *only* on the context.
  6. Answer is streamed back to the user with source links.

Sample System Prompt

You are a helpful and friendly assistant for [Brand Name].
Your goal is to answer user questions based ONLY on the provided context.
Do not make up information. If the answer is not in the context, say "I'm not sure, but I can connect you with our team."
Always cite your sources by including the URL from the metadata.

4) QA Test Plan

Design Notes

  • Chunking strategy: Start with 512 tokens and 50 token overlap. Adjust based on answer quality.
  • Metadata is key: Always store the source URL with each chunk to allow for citations.
  • System Prompt: Be very specific. Tell the AI its name, personality, and what it should *not* do.
  • Fallback logic: If vector search returns no results or a low score, always trigger a "I don't know" response. Never let the LLM answer without context.

Security, Privacy, Compliance

  • Do not ingest Personally Identifiable Information (PII) into the knowledge base.
  • Add a disclaimer in the chat widget that conversations may be monitored.
  • Implement rate limiting on the chat endpoint to prevent abuse.
  • Sanitize user input to prevent prompt injection attacks.

Go‑Live Checklist

  • Widget loads without impacting site performance (LCP).
  • Welcome and fallback messages are approved by the client.
  • Lead capture form is correctly sending data to the webhook.
  • Analytics are tracking chat sessions and conversions.
  • A kill-switch or feature flag is in place to disable the bot instantly.

Audit & Monitoring (Ops)

Key Metrics to Monitor

  • Containment Rate: % of chats without human escalation. Target: >70%.
  • Conversion Rate: % of chats that result in a lead. Target: >5%.
  • Thumbs Up / Thumbs Down Rate: Simple CSAT proxy. Target: >80% thumbs up.
  • API Error Rate: LLM or Vector DB API errors. Alert if > 1%.

Weekly Review

  • Review unhandled questions and identify gaps in the knowledge base.
  • Check conversations with low CSAT scores to find areas for improvement.
  • Update the knowledge base with any new content from the client's site.
