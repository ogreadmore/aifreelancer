⚡ Core Tier — AI Website Chatbot

Minimal integrations, fast setup, small footprint. Designed to launch quickly with reliable RAG and basic lead capture.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We build a helpful assistant for your website using your public content. We start with a small set of key pages, design a concise system prompt with safety guardrails, soft‑launch on a single page, and then deploy sitewide. You’ll approve a 1‑page blueprint before we build, and you’ll see weekly updates and a monthly KPI snapshot.

  • Gather knowledge: Select 10–30 high‑value pages (FAQs, pricing, services).
  • Design behavior: Concise system prompt; clear fallbacks when unsure.
  • Build & test: RAG pipeline + lightweight widget; simple eval set.
  • Launch: Soft‑launch on one page, monitor, then roll out sitewide.
Newbie guide: How this feels end‑to‑end

Story mode: You will pick the most important pages on the site, tell the bot to use only those pages, and try a few questions on a test page. If answers are fuzzy, you’ll add or refine content or the rules. When it looks good, you’ll launch on one page, watch it closely, and then turn it on for the whole site.

  1. We pick the pages most people ask about (FAQs, pricing, product).
  2. We set rules: the bot must use those pages and cite them; if unsure, it says so.
  3. We add the chat widget to a test page, try questions, and fix weak answers.
  4. We launch on one page for 1–2 days (hypercare), then turn it on sitewide.

Example chat

User: Do you offer refunds?
Bot: Yes. Per our refund policy [1], you can request a refund within 30 days.
     Learn more: https://example.com/refunds
User: Can I talk to a person?
Bot: Absolutely — share your email and we’ll follow up, or use our contact form.
  • Success criteria: Containment ≥70%, conversion ≥5%, CSAT ≥4.5, hallucination <3%.
  • Risks: Missing pages → we add them; unclear answers → we adjust the prompt.

💬 Communication & Reporting

Weekly status email; simple 1‑page blueprint after discovery; end‑of‑month KPI snapshot. We keep it plain‑English and visual.

  • Blueprint: 1‑page plain‑English plan after discovery. Approval gates build.
  • Cadence: Weekly updates (Done, Next, Risks/Needs). Slack/Email channel agreed.
  • Visibility: Lightweight Kanban board (To‑Do/Doing/Done). Client access.
  • Reporting: Monthly KPI snapshot (containment, conversion, CSAT, costs).
  • Decision log: Tiny log of key choices with dates to avoid confusion later.
Newbie guide: What do we report?

Story mode: You will collect a few simple charts (containment, conversion, CSAT), note what improved and what needs work, and list three specific changes we’ll make next. Keep it to one page so it’s easy to read.

Step 1 — Kickoff & Goals

Newbie guide: What happens in kickoff?

Story mode: You will ask for the sitemap, list the 10–30 most important pages, agree on the goal (“answer questions fast and capture leads”), and write a one‑page plan in plain English that the client approves.

  • Primary goal: usually “answer questions quickly and capture qualified leads”.
  • KPIs: containment (bot solves without a human), conversion (leads), CSAT (thumbs up), and hallucination rate.
  • Inputs needed: sitemap URL, 10–30 most important pages, brand voice (friendly? formal?), words to avoid.
  • Risks & out of scope: no pricing not on your pricing page; no legal/medical advice.

Step 2 — Knowledge Base

Newbie guide: What is a knowledge base?

Story mode: You will open each key page, copy the useful text (skip nav/footers), split it into small chunks, and upload those chunks to a special search database so the bot can find the right pieces when it answers.

  • Chunking: break long pages into ~512‑token chunks with small overlaps so nothing important is cut off.
  • De‑dupe: remove duplicate text (e.g., repeated footers) before indexing.
  • Embeddings: a numeric representation of meaning; we store those vectors for fast retrieval.
  • Sources: we keep the original URL so answers can link back for trust.

Step 3 — Design & Prompting

Newbie guide: How does the bot stay on‑brand and safe?

Story mode: You will write a short set of rules: use provided context only, cite sources, say “I’m not sure” if unsure, and escalate to a human. You’ll include brand tone (“friendly, concise”).

A system prompt sets the bot’s permanent rules (tone, sourcing, refusals). Conversation flows define the happy path (greeting → answer → offer to help) and what to do when uncertain.

  • Guardrails: “Use only provided context; if unsure, say so and offer a human follow‑up.”
  • Retrieval budget: fetch 3–5 top chunks; don’t over‑retrieve (slows answers, increases noise).
  • Lead capture: ask once per session after value is delivered; collect name + email only.
  • Escalation: provide a clear path to a human (contact form or mailto link).

Step 4 — Build & Integrate

Newbie guide: How does it get on the site?

Story mode: You will paste a small embed code into one test page (or a staging site), ask a few common questions, and verify the bot links to the right pages. Only then will you add it across the site.

  • Staging first: test on a hidden page before going sitewide.
  • Privacy: show a note that conversations may be logged to improve answers.
  • Fallbacks: ensure a “Contact us” link is always available.

Step 5 — Testing

Newbie guide: How do we test it?

Story mode: You will make a list of 20–30 typical questions and a few tricky ones, run them, and mark each answer as correct, needs work, or unsafe. You’ll fix the weak spots and re‑test.

  • Acceptance: ≥70% containment, hallucination <3% on the eval set.
  • Evidence: keep screenshots/links; update the knowledge base where answers were weak.
  • Safety: confirm the bot cites the pricing page and avoids speculative claims.

Step 6 — Launch & Hypercare

Newbie guide: What is hypercare?

Story mode: You will watch the bot closely for the first 1–2 days, fix any wrong answers or missing citations, and only then enable it on the rest of the site.

  • Watch for: broken links, wrong citations, high error rates, odd conversations.
  • Rollout plan: turn it on for the rest of the site in one click after checks pass.

Step 7 — Ongoing Optimization

Newbie guide: How do we keep it sharp?

Story mode: Each week you will review a few conversations, add missing Q&A to the knowledge base, and adjust the prompt or retrieval settings if needed. Each month you will share a one‑page results summary and next steps.

Every week we add missing answers to the knowledge base and keep a short list of improvements. Each month we share a very small deck of results and next steps.

  • Backlog: log common failures; add/refresh pages that would help.
  • Metrics: show trend lines for containment, conversion, and CSAT.

🛡️ Security & Compliance

  • PII minimization: name/email only if user volunteers; avoid sensitive fields.
  • Storage: logs retained 30–60 days; redact emails in exports.
  • Keys: store in secrets manager; never in code or docs.
Newbie guide: What is PII?

Personally Identifiable Information. Collect only what’s needed to follow up, nothing more.

Story mode: You will only ask for email (and name if needed). You won’t store sensitive info. You will set a short retention period for logs and be ready to export or delete data on request.

🚀 Enhanced Tier — AI Website Chatbot

Guided flows, CRM/webhook integration, analytics dashboard, optional multilingual.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We extend Core with richer flows and integrations. We ingest PDFs/docs, map fields to the CRM, add guided intents with validation, and provide a dashboard for insights. We run UAT with 30–50 cases before launch and include retries/backoff so lead creation is reliable.

  • Guided paths: pricing, product, and contact flows steer users to the right outcome.
  • CRM/webhook: we send clean data with idempotency to avoid duplicates.
  • Analytics: track conversion by intent and top questions; share monthly deck.
Newbie guide: How Enhanced differs from Core

Story mode: You will add guided paths (menus/buttons), connect the bot to your CRM/webhooks with idempotency and retries, and review a dashboard that shows conversion by intent.

  • Field mapping: simple doc that shows exactly what’s sent to the CRM.
  • UAT plan: 30–50 test cases, including errors (5xx/429) and timeouts.
  • Multilingual (optional): detect language; fallback to English if unsure.

💬 Communication & Reporting

Weekly updates; CRM integration change log; monthly deck with conversion by intent and lead quality signals.

  • Dashboard link and notes on top intents/questions driving conversions.
  • UAT plan, schedule, and sign‑off checklist before launch.
  • Error budget and retry/backoff settings documented for clarity.
Newbie guide: What’s in the monthly deck?

Story mode: You will export a few charts (conversion by intent, lead quality tags, top questions), add two bullets about wins and two about fixes, and list three concrete next actions for the month.

Step 1 — Discovery & CRM

Newbie guide: CRM discovery and intents

Story mode: You will list the top questions users ask, decide which ones should create leads, and gather the API key or webhook URL. You will also pick what fields (name, email, tags) to send for each intent.

  • Field mapping doc: shows every field (name, email, phone, notes, tags) and how we fill it.
  • Scopes/permissions: we request the smallest possible CRM permissions to create/read leads.
  • Multilingual: if enabled, pick locales and default to English when unsure.

Step 2 — Knowledge + Docs

Newbie guide: Adding PDFs and documents

Story mode: You will choose final public documents, extract the text (OCR if needed), store them as chunks with a link to the exact page, and spot‑check that citations go to the right place.

  • Prefer final, public versions to avoid wrong or draft info.
  • Check random samples in the dashboard to confirm citations point to the right doc page.

Step 3 — Flows & Prompting

Newbie guide: Intent routing and validation

Story mode: You will define a small menu (“pricing, product, contact”), send users into the right path, and validate inputs (email format, consent) before submitting anything.

  • Router: classify intent (pricing, product, support) → pick the right prompt template.
  • Validation: if email looks invalid, ask politely to re‑enter; tag leads by intent.
  • Dedup: avoid duplicate CRM entries by hashing email or checking recent leads.

Step 4 — Build & Integrate

Newbie guide: Webhooks and retries

Story mode: You will send a JSON payload to your CRM/webhook with an idempotency key. If the server is busy (429/5xx), you will try again after 1s, then 2s, then 4s, and stop after 3 tries.

  • Idempotency: include a unique request ID so repeating the call doesn’t create duplicates.
  • Backoff: wait a little longer after each failure (e.g., 1s, 2s, 4s, up to a limit).
  • Sandbox first: test on a CRM sandbox or test list before touching production.

Step 5 — UAT & Testing

Newbie guide: What is UAT?

Story mode: You will ask a few teammates or the client to try the bot using a test plan. If they find issues, you fix them, re‑test, and then you sign off to launch.

  • Include success and failure paths (timeouts, rate limits, wrong inputs).
  • Record observed conversion by intent and any content gaps.
  • Exit when acceptance criteria are met (e.g., ≥75% task success in UAT).

Step 6 — Launch & Optimize

Newbie guide: After launch

Story mode: You will set alerts for errors and latency, review top intents weekly, and update guided prompts to improve clarity. Each month you’ll share a short deck with trends and next steps.

  • Set alerts for webhook errors and prolonged high latency.
  • Review top intents and reword guided prompts for clarity.
  • Share a simple monthly deck with trends and next steps.

🏢 Enterprise Tier — AI Website Chatbot

Multi‑source RAG, advanced safety/PII, SSO admin, observability, and change management.

✨ Quick Story — What will happen

We integrate internal data sources (APIs/DBs) alongside public content with strict grounding and citations. Admin access is protected by SSO and roles (RBAC). We ship with dev/stage/prod, feature flags, and deep observability, and we roll out gradually (canary) with a fast rollback if needed. Security and compliance (SOC2/GDPR) are baked in from day one.

  • Connectors: scheduled indexers with lineage, backfills, and controls.
  • Safety: confidence thresholds; show citations; refuse when uncertain.
  • Operations: on‑call runbooks, SLO dashboards, incident response, and RCAs.
Newbie guide: What makes Enterprise different?

Story mode: You will connect to internal systems safely, require sign‑in for admin changes, ship behind flags, and watch health (SLOs) so rollouts are predictable and reversible.

  1. Data at scale: we connect to internal APIs/DBs, refresh on schedules, and keep track of where answers came from.
  2. Safety first: if the bot isn’t confident, it asks the user to clarify or routes to a human.
  3. Change control: all risky changes require approval; we ship behind feature flags.
  4. Progressive rollout: we start at 5% of traffic (canary), then 25%, then 100% if health is good.
  5. Reliability: a person is on‑call; incidents have runbooks and post‑mortems to prevent repeats.

Example canary (conceptual)

Stage 1: 5% traffic for 2h → error rate < 2% and p95 latency < target?
Stage 2: 25% for 6h → same checks + conversion stable?
Stage 3: 100% → monitor for 24h; keep rollback ready.

Outcome: A reliable assistant that answers from trusted sources, protects sensitive data, and evolves safely with clear ownership.

💬 Communication & Reporting

SLO dashboards for availability/latency; on‑call rotations; incident runbooks and RCAs; monthly evals; quarterly red‑team reviews.

  • Change calendar with approvals; weekly governance notes and owners.
  • Security reviews and red‑team findings tracked to closure.
  • Cost and error budgets reported alongside product outcomes.
Newbie guide: SLOs vs SLIs vs SLAs

SLIs are measurements (like latency). SLOs are the internal targets. SLAs are external promises with consequences. We track SLIs, set SLOs, and sometimes publish SLAs.

Story mode: You will choose 2–3 SLIs (latency, error rate, availability), set achievable SLO targets, and add alerts if they drift out of bounds.

Step 1 — Discovery & Governance

Newbie guide: Why governance matters

Story mode: You will identify owners (product, security), write down who can change what, and require a second reviewer for risky changes.

Enterprises need clear owners, guardrails, and targets. We define service level objectives (SLOs), who can change what, and how to roll back changes safely.

  • Stakeholders: product, security, data, and support.
  • Maker‑checker: risky changes require approval by a second person.
  • Risk register: list risks (data quality, outages) with mitigations and owners.

Step 2 — Data & Safety

Newbie guide: Connectors and safety nets

Story mode: You will connect to APIs/DBs in read‑safe ways, schedule refreshes, and always show citations so users can see where answers come from.

  • Schedules: nightly or hourly indexing, with backfills for missed runs.
  • Lineage: track which data source produced which answer for audits.
  • DLP: block sensitive data from entering prompts or logs.

Step 3 — Platform & Observability

Newbie guide: Why multiple environments?

Story mode: You will try changes in dev, confirm in stage, and then enable in prod using a feature flag. If anything looks off, you’ll flip the flag off.

  • Observability: logs (what happened), traces (where time went), metrics (numbers over time), costs (per conversation).
  • Admin portal: protected by SSO + RBAC so only the right people can change behavior.

Step 4 — Testing & Releases

Newbie guide: Safe releases

Story mode: You will test new prompts/features with 5% of users (canary), confirm health and conversion are steady, then roll out to 25% and 100%. You’ll keep a rollback switch ready.

  • A/B: compare two versions; keep the better one.
  • Freeze windows: avoid risky releases during peak business times.

Step 5 — Run & Resilience

Newbie guide: Running it day‑to‑day

Story mode: You will be on‑call for alerts, fix incidents fast using runbooks, keep evaluation packs to track quality, and run periodic red‑team tests to improve safety.

Operating the bot means watching SLOs, fixing incidents fast, and continually improving quality. We document who responds and what to check first.

  • On‑call: alerts route to humans; drills confirm we can respond quickly.
  • Eval packs: a stable set of test questions to track quality over time.
  • Red‑team: periodic “try to break it” exercises to improve safety.

💡 Design Notes

  • Strict grounding: confidence threshold; cite sources; suppress low‑confidence answers.
  • Rollout: canary by segment, feature flags, reversible config.
  • Access controls: SSO/RBAC for admin UI; maker‑checker for risky changes.

🛡️ Security & Compliance

  • Compliance: SOC2/GDPR mapping; DLP scans; encryption at rest/in transit.
  • Incident response: runbooks, RTO/RPO targets, post‑incident RCAs.
  • Data governance: change approvals; data classification; retention schedules.
