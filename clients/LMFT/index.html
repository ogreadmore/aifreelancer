This document is the complete launch package for a California LMFT starting a private practice with a focus on fertility, pregnancy, and postpartum mental health. It covers everything from the legal scaffolding to the words on the door. It's organized in two parts.

Part I — The Launch Plan walks through the compliance, legal, and tax foundation most formation guides skip entirely. The through-line in both parts: start lean, verify twice, spend once, build something that ages well.

Why not incorporate right away? Even though California waives the minimum franchise tax in a corporation's first tax year, from year two onward you owe $800/year regardless of profit — plus CPA fees and payroll costs. A sole proprietor with malpractice insurance and a filed FBN is fully legitimate and costs almost nothing to operate. Incorporating makes sense once your net profit is high enough that S-Corp tax savings (~$5–10k/year) comfortably outweigh ongoing CPA and payroll costs (~$2–4k/year). That crossover typically lands around $60–80k net profit per year.

§2 · Phase 0 · Before You See Your First Client

Before you see a single client — regardless of whether you're a sole proprietor or a corporation — a short list of non-negotiable pieces needs to be in place. Some are legally required (HIPAA, federal Good Faith Estimates, California fee disclosure). Some are practical baseline (malpractice insurance, a compliant EHR). None of these should be skipped. Most formation guides ignore this layer entirely, which is how therapists end up incorporated but out of compliance.

1
Licensing

Confirm your full license status

California is strict: you cannot own or operate a private practice if you are still in your associate registration period. Confirm your LMFT license is fully active with the California Board of Behavioral Sciences (BBS) before proceeding. Everything downstream depends on this.

2
Insurance

Bind malpractice insurance before your first session

As a sole proprietor, malpractice insurance isn't supplementary — it's your primary liability protection. Bind coverage before you see a single client.

  • Standard carriers for therapists: CPH & Associates and HPSO — both are considered gold-standard for LMFTs
  • Typical cost: $200–400/year for $1M per occurrence / $3M aggregate — standard limits for outpatient private practice
  • Confirm the policy includes telehealth coverage and covers any states where you see clients virtually
  • Re-evaluate coverage limits and carrier when you incorporate in Phase 2
3
Clinical Infrastructure

Build a HIPAA-compliant practice stack

Every tool that touches protected health information (PHI) must be HIPAA-compliant and backed by a signed Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with the vendor. Standard consumer tools — regular Gmail, consumer Zoom, iMessage, Google Docs without a workspace BAA — are not compliant and will create liability.

EHR / Practice Mgmt
SimplePractice, TherapyNotes, or Jane

Notes, scheduling, billing, telehealth, and client portal in one. ~$60–100/month. All three offer BAAs.

Email
Google Workspace + BAA, Hushmail, or Paubox

Never use personal Gmail for PHI. Google Workspace requires the BAA signed separately. ~$6–25/month.

Telehealth
Built into your EHR (preferred)

If you need a standalone option, use Zoom for Healthcare (not consumer Zoom) or Doxy.me — both offer BAAs.

Phone / Fax
Spruce Health or Google Voice (Workspace)

A dedicated business line is non-negotiable. Don't give out your personal cell. ~$25–40/month.

📄

Get a BAA from every vendor. HIPAA requires a written Business Associate Agreement with any service that handles PHI on your behalf. Keep signed copies in a dedicated folder — if you're ever audited or sued, this is the first thing anyone will ask for.

4
Identifiers

Get your NPI and EIN

Two free federal identifiers that you'll use on every intake form, superbill, and insurance claim — better to have them than use your Social Security number on client paperwork.

  • NPI Type 1 (Individual) — free at NPPES (nppes.cms.hhs.gov). Takes about 10 minutes. Get this now regardless of whether you plan to take insurance.
  • NPI Type 2 (Organization) — apply for this after you incorporate in Phase 2, under the corporation's name.
  • EIN — free from the IRS (irs.gov). Use this instead of your SSN on all business and client paperwork.
💡

Insurance vs. private pay is a separate strategic decision. Credentialing with insurance panels takes 3–6 months and typically pays $70–110/session; private pay in your niche often runs $180–250+. You don't need to decide today, but decide before you open the calendar — it affects your rates, intake process, and whether you'll issue superbills for out-of-network reimbursement.

5
Compliance Paperwork

Set up your required disclosures and consent forms

California and federal law require a specific set of written disclosures that every client must receive. Most EHRs (SimplePractice, TherapyNotes) have templates you can customize — but the responsibility for correctness is yours, not the software's.

  • Informed consent & fee disclosure — California requires fees to be disclosed in writing at the outset of treatment, along with your license type, license number, and the BBS's contact information
  • Notice of Privacy Practices (NPP) — HIPAA requires this be provided at first contact. Free template available from HHS (hhs.gov)
  • Good Faith Estimate (GFE) — required under the federal No Surprises Act since 2022. Every self-pay client must receive a written estimate of expected charges before treatment begins. Set up a repeatable workflow in your EHR
  • Telehealth consent — a separate form confirming the client understands the limits and risks of virtual sessions
  • Records retention policy — California requires clinical records be kept for 7 years after last date of service (or until a minor client turns 25, whichever is longer)
§3 · Phase 1 · Launch Lean
6
Legal Structure · Phase 1

Start as a sole proprietor

When you're just launching, a sole proprietorship is the right call. There is no formation paperwork, no $800/year franchise tax, and no corporate compliance overhead. You simply practice, file income on your personal tax return (Schedule C), and focus on building a client base.

💡

When to upgrade to a corporation: Once your net profit is consistently around $60,000–$80,000/year, incorporating becomes a tax-smart move. Below that threshold, the costs outweigh the savings.

7
Brand Name

File your public-facing brand name

Whether you're a sole proprietor or a corporation, the name patients see should be filed as a Fictitious Business Name (FBN) with the county. Temecula is in Riverside County — not San Diego County.

→

For the name itself, see Part II of this document. The working hypothesis is Camellia Perinatal Therapy. Because Parker Perinatal Therapy has direct perinatal conflicts (see §7, #1), the founder-named option is not an automatic safety fallback — if Camellia fails §10 checks, she should pivot to Hearth Perinatal Therapy or Aster Perinatal Therapy rather than defaulting to her surname. Full research in §7 · The Shortlist.

  • Finalize the name (run the Lock-In Checklist in §10)
  • File the FBN Statement with Riverside County Clerk within 40 days of first doing business. Fee: ~$26
  • Publish in an approved local newspaper once a week for four consecutive weeks after filing. Cost: ~$50–100
  • File the affidavit of publication with Riverside County to complete the process
  • Open a dedicated business bank account in your FBN name — keeps personal and business finances cleanly separated from day one
8
Local Licensing

Register for your City of Temecula business license

The City of Temecula requires a business license for anyone operating a business within city limits — including home-based therapy practices. This is separate from your state license and your county FBN. It's cheap, fast, and frequently overlooked.

  • Apply through the City of Temecula Finance Department (temeculaca.gov) — can be done online
  • Annual fee is modest — typically $35–75/year for a professional services business
  • Apply after your FBN is filed so the business name on the license matches
  • If seeing clients in a home office, verify zoning compliance — Temecula permits most home-based professional practices, but confirm with the Planning Department before advertising your address
  • Renew annually — the city will send a renewal notice to the address on file
§4 · Phase 2 · When Revenue Justifies It
9
Legal Structure · Phase 2

Incorporate as a Professional Corporation

California does not allow therapists to use an LLC. When it's time to incorporate, you'll form a Professional Corporation (PC). The corporate name must contain one of marriage / family / child plus one of therapy / therapist / counseling / counselor.

Parker Family Therapy, A Professional Corporation Recommended Legal Entity Name

File Articles of Incorporation at bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov. You can reserve the name for 60 days before filing. Your existing FBN brand names carry over — just re-file them under the new corporate entity within 40 days.

  • File Articles of Incorporation — California Secretary of State. Fee: $100
  • File Statement of Information within 90 days of formation, then annually
  • Re-file your FBN(s) under the new corporate entity within 40 days of incorporating; update your Temecula city business license to match
  • Apply for your NPI Type 2 (organizational) under the corporation's name
  • Update all client disclosures: California law requires you inform clients at the outset of treatment that services are provided by a marriage and family therapy corporation
⚠️

Franchise tax timing: California exempts corporations from the $800 minimum franchise tax in their first taxable year — but starting year two, it's charged regardless of profit. S-corps specifically owe the greater of $800 or 1.5% of net income. Time your incorporation with this in mind: incorporating late in a calendar year can buy you a nearly-free first year.

10
Tax Strategy

Elect S-Corp status — this is where the real savings are

By default a PC is taxed as a C-Corp: the business pays tax, then you pay tax again on distributions. An S-Corp election fixes this. It's not a different company — it's a tax status you elect by filing one IRS form, and it can save you thousands every year.

PC Without S-Corp
Default C-Corp Taxation

Profits are taxed at the corporate level, then again when distributed to you personally. Even if you avoid this by taking everything as salary, you pay payroll tax on every dollar of net profit.

With S-Corp Election
PC + S-Corp Status

You pay yourself a reasonable salary — only that portion is subject to payroll tax. The rest comes out as distributions, not subject to SE tax. Typical annual savings: $5,000–$10,000.

  • File IRS Form 2553 to elect S-Corp status — ideally before January 1 of the tax year you want it to apply, or within the first 75 days of the year
  • Set up payroll through a provider like Gusto or ADP — you'll pay yourself a salary as an employee of your own corporation
  • Work with a CPA experienced with therapy practices — your salary must be "reasonable" by IRS standards or it raises red flags. For a full-time clinician, this typically lands at 50–65% of net profit, not 30%
11
Tax Optimization Layer

Stack the rest of the tax-advantaged moves

Once you're an S-Corp paying yourself a salary, a few additional pieces multiply the savings. These are standard hygiene for a well-run corporate practice — skipping them leaves real money on the table.

  • Retirement vehicle. A Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA inside the S-Corp can shelter an additional $20,000–$60,000+/year depending on salary. At higher incomes this often outweighs the S-Corp election savings themselves
  • Accountable plan. Adopt a written policy that lets the corporation reimburse you tax-free for legitimate business expenses — home office, mileage, phone, CEUs, licensure, books. Costs almost nothing to set up; losing this is a common mistake
  • EDD registration. Once you run payroll, register with the California Employment Development Department as an employer
  • Workers' comp exemption. California requires workers' comp coverage for corporate employees, but sole corporate officers can file the exemption form (Officer Exclusion, via your WC carrier) to opt out — saving ~$500–1,500/year
  • QBI deduction awareness. Therapy is a "Specified Service Trade or Business" under IRS Section 199A, so the 20% Qualified Business Income deduction phases out above certain income thresholds. Your CPA should model this into your salary decision — it's not a fire-and-forget setting
  • Self-employed health insurance. Premiums can be run through the corporation and deducted — another standard S-Corp move

Part II

Brand Name Research

Five ranked candidates, the names that didn't survive availability research, and the checklist to run before anything gets filed.

Any good practice name has to clear four bars at once: niche fit (does it resonate with a woman searching for fertility, pregnancy, or postpartum support?), distinctiveness (is it already owned by another perinatal practice?), emotional resonance (does it feel safe and warm to someone in a vulnerable state?), and practical availability (is the domain, trademark class, and social handle clear enough to build a brand on?).

The research below started with roughly fifty candidates across every register I could reach — metaphorical (plants, seasons, light, water, threads, architectural, astronomical), clinical-warm, literary, founder-named, bilingual, place-based, and beautiful-first — and checked each against existing California and national perinatal practices, USPTO concepts, and directory listings. The short version: "Bloom" is unusable in this niche. "BLOOM Maternal Therapy" already operates in California with near-identical positioning. Twenty-two names survived the filter — five as the core Shortlist (§7), plus seventeen more in §8: four viable alternatives, two honorable mentions, three beautiful-first candidates, four more beautiful candidates, and four from continued search on the beautiful axis. Three earlier candidates (Motherline, The Tending, The Honey Room) were removed per user feedback. A note on the founder-named option: Parker Perinatal Therapy (#2) is not the bulletproof fallback that an unusual surname would provide — Parker-Shames Counseling is a PMH-C perinatal practice with adjacent branding, so the "founder-named = automatic safety" heuristic doesn't hold here. Details in the #2 card.

Camellia Perinatal Therapy

The camellia blooms in winter when almost nothing else does — a flower that insists on beauty during the hardest season. That is the practice, in a single image.

Why it works. Three-syllable musical word (cuh-MEE-lee-uh). Reads as unmistakably feminine and elegant without feeling precious. The camellia's symbolism maps almost too cleanly onto perinatal work: it's the flower that holds the winter — that finds a way to bloom through dormancy, loss, or hardship. That maps onto fertility struggles, pregnancy loss, postpartum recovery, and the slow re-emergence of self after birth. The name passes the three-bar test most perinatal brands fail: it's beautiful on first read, meaningful on second read, and distinctive in a floral-saturated niche.

What the research showed. No direct perinatal therapy practice using "Camellia" in this niche surfaced in national searches. Less saturated than the big-five florals (Bloom, Willow, Dahlia, Magnolia, Wisteria). USPTO Class 44 clearance is still recommended before filing, but initial availability signals are strong.

2 Founder-Named
Founder-named · Professional

Parker Perinatal Therapy

Her name on the door. Personal, accountable, and grown-up — but Parker is a common surname, which means this option requires more careful trademark clearance than most founder-named practices would.

Why it works. Therapy is an intensely personal relationship, and many successful solo practices are named after the clinician. Pairing "Parker" with "Perinatal Therapy" makes the niche legible, signals personal accountability, and flexes if she later adds a second clinician or shifts focus. The name reads as credible to referring OB/GYNs and insurance panels.

What the research showed — important caveats. Parker is a common English surname, and the therapy space is partially claimed. Two adjacencies matter: Parker-Shames Counseling is a PMH-C certified perinatal practice (exact-specialty overlap); Jennifer Parker, LCSW at CaroMont Health does reproductive and perinatal work. Neither is California-based and neither uses "Parker Perinatal Therapy" as their exact brand, but both occupy adjacent SEO and directory space. There are also several generic Parker-named therapy practices (Parker Counseling Services in Parker, CO; Parker Counseling; Family Care Center Parker). This is not a bulletproof fallback the way an uncommon surname would be.

5 Evocative
Metaphorical · Liminal

Threshold Perinatal Therapy

Fertility, pregnancy, and postpartum as the threshold — the doorway between one version of yourself and the next.

Why it works. Matrescence (the clinical term for becoming a mother) is fundamentally a liminal experience — and "threshold" is the exact word for that kind of crossing. It's a real clinical-literary concept, used by writers like Aviva Romm and in ritual-studies language. It would be an unusually sophisticated brand for this niche.

Why it landed at #5. One subtle issue: in perinatal clinical literature, "threshold of viability" refers to the earliest gestational age at which a baby can survive outside the womb. For clients who have experienced preterm birth, miscarriage, or infant loss — a meaningful slice of a fertility-focused practice — that association could land wrong. Most clients won't know this technical meaning, but the ones most likely to read it through that lens are her most vulnerable.

8 Clinical
Clinical · Elegant

Maternal Arc Therapy

The maternal arc is the full line a woman walks — from wanting a child, to carrying one, to becoming a mother.

Why it works. "Arc" does what most perinatal branding fails to do — it names the narrative shape of the experience. There's a beginning, a middle, an emotional trajectory. §10 · Lock-In Checklist

Once she picks a finalist, these are the verifications to run — in this order — before paying for the FBN filing, the domain, or a logo designer. A rename three months in costs weeks of rework; a rename three years in is catastrophic.

  • Psychology Today directory search. Search the exact name in the California therapist directory. This is where 90% of clients will search — if an identical or near-identical name appears, clients will confuse you.
  • Google search: "[Name] therapy California" and "[Name] perinatal." Look at page one only — that's where clients look. If page one has a similar practice, move on.
  • USPTO TESS search (tmsearch.uspto.gov) in International Class 44 (medical and therapy services). Look for live marks — dead or abandoned marks are usually not a concern.
  • California Secretary of State business name search (bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov) for any existing corporate entity using the name.
  • Riverside County FBN database for any local filed fictitious business names.
  • Domain check. Ideally the exact .com is available. If not, a short clean variant (e.g., camelliaperinatal.com, hearthperinatal.com) is acceptable. Avoid hyphens and cute Tlds like .co or .love — they read as amateur.
  • Instagram handle check. @[name] or @[name]therapy — this matters for content marketing. If a dormant account is sitting on the handle, consider a variant rather than fighting for it.
  • PSI Directory check (psidirectory.com — Postpartum Support International). This is a key referral directory for perinatal clients; if a similarly-named practice is listed there, you'll be permanently downstream of them.
  • Say it out loud to three people. Literally say "Hi, I'm calling from ___" and "___, how can I help?" If it feels awkward in her mouth, or if her test audience can't spell it back to her, try another.

Names that win popular votes tend to be the most generic ones — which is exactly how the market ended up full of Blooms and Willows. Pick a name that feels right to her and survives the availability checks above.
