Recovery

How to think about recovery after a long run.

The first 24 hours after hard miles are about calming the system, checking in honestly, and giving sore tissue a better environment to settle.

May 2026 5 min read

Quick frame

This is general recovery education, not medical advice. Sharp, unusual, or worsening pain deserves a clinician.

Recovery Services

A long run asks a lot from your legs, but the recovery plan does not need to be dramatic. Think of it in three parts: downshift, restore, and reassess.

Downshift first.

Right after the run, the goal is to bring your system back toward baseline. Walk for a few minutes, get out of wet gear, sip fluids, and eat something with carbohydrate and protein. That is the boring work that makes the rest of the day easier.

Restore before you chase more stimulus.

Stretching, compression, massage, and cold therapy can all have a place, but they work best after the basics are handled. Hydration, food, and sleep still do the heavy lifting.

Where localized cold fits:

Localized cryotherapy can be useful when a specific area feels hot, irritated, or stubbornly sore. Knees, calves, hips, ankles, and feet are common targets after long miles.

Reassess the next morning.

Normal soreness usually changes once you warm up. Pain that gets sharper, shifts your gait, or lingers in one exact spot is a different conversation. Take that seriously and get the right professional eyes on it.

Keep the plan repeatable.

The best recovery routine is the one you can actually repeat. Simple beats heroic: fluids, food, light movement, sleep, and targeted support when a specific area needs help.
