02 · History

It belongs to everyone

Buddhism preserved the Metta Sutta and carried it across the world — and the practice it gathers is older still. Three thousand years ago, Indian voices were already asking to see every being with the eye of a friend. The history is an invitation: this love has always belonged to anyone willing to practice it.

The method

One practice, four movements

1

Find it

Bring to mind the being who evokes the strongest feeling of love in you. No anchor is too humble — the love you can actually feel is worth more than the love you think you should feel.

2

Strengthen it

Give the feeling your full attention and turn it all the way up. Think of metta as a mantra you feel rather than say.

3

Expand it

Keep the same felt wish and let it include more: yourself, a neutral person, a difficult person, and finally all beings — above, below, and all around.

4

Return to it

Standing, walking, sitting, lying down. When you notice it has slipped away, return. Each return makes it more familiar, until it is simply there.

Morning: twenty minutes. During the day: countless returns. Night: five minutes in bed.

What the mind rehearses becomes easier to find.

Is this a religious book?

No religious commitment is required or requested. The book treats the Metta Sutta as what it is — a Buddhist poem gathering a practice older than Buddhism — and teaches the practice in plain language. Readers of any tradition, and of none, practice from the same pages.

I've never meditated. Can I start here?

Yes — the book assumes nothing. It was written to be the first book, readable in an evening, with a practice that begins at one sincere minute.

What does the research actually show?

Chapter 1 reads the evidence honestly: studies suggest loving-kindness practice can increase positive emotion, soften self-criticism, support people under stress, and improve social connection. The book neither hypes the findings nor hides their limits — every source in its 39 endnotes was verified against its publisher.
