The science

The history

Buddhism preserved the Metta Sutta, chanted it for centuries, and carried it across the world. The practice it gathers is older still — the prayer above was spoken in India centuries before the Buddha. The history is an invitation: this love has always belonged to anyone willing to practice it.

The art

The Sutta itself, in the author's own translation, read line by line — then one practice: find it, strengthen it, expand it, return to it. It scales to any life: one minute, five minutes, twenty. Morning, countless small returns, and five minutes in bed at night.

The author

Taylor Oliphant is a long-time metta practitioner, trained primarily in the tradition of Ajahn Sona of Birken Forest Monastery, whose full metta retreats shaped the feeling-first method in Chapter 4. He lived for several years in Nepal, where the daily overlap of Buddhist and Hindu practice first convinced him that metta belongs to everyone. The book's translation of the Metta Sutta is his own.

The register is academic and plain: every claim sourced, every quotation verified, nothing oversold. The dedication is to a dog.

Questions

Is this a religious book?

No religious commitment is required or requested. The book treats the Metta Sutta as what it is — a Buddhist poem gathering a practice older than Buddhism — and teaches the practice in plain language. Readers of any tradition, and of none, practice from the same pages.

I've never meditated. Can I start here?

Yes — the book assumes nothing. It was written to be the first book, readable in an evening, with a practice that begins at one sincere minute.

Why is the whole book free to read?
